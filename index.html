01 · Where we are — said plainly

A cash-timing problem, more than a business problem.

Our runway is exhausted next month.

Block Aero has cultivated a clear pipeline of $1.6M in annual recurring revenue against a growing book of signed and quoted accounts as the first blockchain solution ever mandated by a regulator in China — the AFRA-CAAC parts registry. The product is live and the pipeline is real.

What we are short of is the bridge between the burn we have already absorbed and the revenue that arrives over the next two to three quarters.

Our H2 2026 gross operating spend is ~$795K. But the number that matters is net burn, because revenue offsets it month by month. In our base case, net burn from July collapses from the high-$60Ks per month to roughly breakeven by year-end as the Q4 onboarding wave lands.

02 · Why we are not cutting our way out

Preserving the team is cash-superior to dismantling it.

Cutting headcount to extend runway is the wrong move, and the math says so. Our Bangkok team sits under Thai statutory severance, which scales steeply with tenure: reducing it triggers large, immediate cash outflows exactly when we are conserving cash. The net benefit is marginal at best — and not realized until well into 2027, long after the ramp this bridge reaches.

Worse, the people we would cut are the delivery engine for the revenue in this memo: the engineers shipping the AFRA-CAAC registry, the QC station, and the OEM/lessor integrations. Cutting them to save cash would kneecap the very pathways that close the funding gap. That is the disciplined choice, not the sentimental one.

05 · Recapitalization & noteholder protection

The bridge takes the senior position.

The recap is a balance-sheet event, not operating runway. The bridge carries operations to cash-flow positive; the recap repays or converts the existing ~$3M convertible notes (16.5% APR), protecting noteholders and cleaning the balance sheet. The bridge is first money in, and converts into it.

The risks, and what answers them.

Aero-to-Power timing

upside, not load-bearing; the base case stands without it, and it is separately funded.

treated as optionality, never as committed revenue in the bridge math.

Recap execution risk

the bridge buys the full runway to raise it properly, not a distressed sale next month.

08 · The asymmetry

Under $1M secures the largest optionality.

A $500–800K bridge, against a company that turns cash-flow positive in early 2027 — carrying four paths to revenue, a regulator-mandated product, and a recap that protects existing notes. The downside is bounded; the upside includes a stream that alone could cover our burn (Aero-to-Power) and a deal that could reset the revenue base (OEM/lessor).
